Pattern Confirmation for the undecided

You know how sometimes a Yes/No dialog seems kind of overwhelming? Here's a not so serious solution for the issue.

Both answers form a single button, the cursor position on the button is shown as relatve filling and a click has a probability doing one or the other option according to the relative click location on the button.

For example: The cursor 7em from the left of a button with 10em width --> 70% chance for the right option to be executed.

But seriously, what you should do is label your buttons with clear verbs that indicate what the user can do, e.g. "Confirm" & "Cancel".

Fonts, Part 2

This time I'd like to highlight two fonts that catched my eye while watching a rather unknown movie called "Jack and the Cuckoo-Clock Heart" (orig. fr. "Jack et la mécanique du coeur", ger. "Jack und das Kuckucksuhrherz"). The movie was shown at the 64th Berlin International Film Festival and later on released directly to Blu-Ray.

The intro of the movie already starts out with two playful, old-timey but still nicely readable fonts forming the main credits of the production.

Landsdowne

The spectacle before us was indeed sublime.

Summertime

Apparently we had reached a great height in the atmosphere, for the sky was a dead black, and the stars had ceased to twinkle. By the same illusion which lifts the horizon of the sea to the level of the spectator on a hillside, the sable cloud beneath was dished out, and the car seemed to float in the middle of an immense dark sphere, whose upper half was strewn with silver. Looking down into the dark gulf below, I could see a ruddy light streaming through a rift in the clouds.

Landsdowne was created in 2002 by Paul Lloyd

A lightweight decorative Roman with a hint of fun. Think of early 20th century advertising.

Summertime was created in 2001 by Sara (this is an archived link due to the fact that her page went offline in 2016)

This font, Summertime, was originally created for a splash page I never got around to making. Maybe next summer... Like all my fonts, here I have consciously aimed at a handmade look, with rough edges here and there and elements which go together but don't precisely match. This particular font is not a replica of any specific lettering style; it's just what came out of my digital pen while I was thinking about a nostalgic concept. It is meant to evoke the voice of hand-drawn illustration and poster advertising alphabets from the late 19th-early 20th century, but even more the 1960s-70s craft-fair-and-flower-stand hand-rendered revivals of those alphabet styles, which hold special summertime memories for me from my childhood.
